Students fight back!

A brutal clash

Anyone near Clemenceau Avenue, Penang Road on May 13 > would have seen a violent clash outside. This was the tragic National Service Riot, which saw 26 people injured. Students from Chung Cheng High (中正高) and Chinese High School (南洋华侨中学) simply wanted their voices to be heard by the British Administration, but the police handled the students with unreasonable and unforgivable violence.

A poorly handled situation

The riot police’s and the government’s handling of the students were both unacceptable, without which, this fateful event could have been avoided. The students felt that they were being unfairly treated by the British, and wanted to have a voice in the matter.




To understand the cause of the riots, we must look at the National Service Ordinance, which is a policy recently implemented, requiring all men aged 18 to 20 to serve in the military. The government’s reason for this policy: a capable defence force would be needed in a country seeking self-government.
The government ignoring the students caused a detrimental lack of communication which only exacerbated this feeling. The students got increasingly desperate and eventually felt that they needed to protest to get themselves a voice. This might be seen as a radical decision, but we must acknowledge that the government’s negligence was a leading cause for this.

A confrontation of logic

However, the Chinese community was against this decision as not only did it interfere with the students’ education opportunities, but also went against the traditional Chinese belief that good men do not become soldiers. One cannot ignore the fact that throughout Singapore’s history as a British colony, the Chinese community and culture were constantly treated as relatively unimportant by the British. As such, many Chinese did not want to fight for and serve them.

A chaotic scene where the police can be seen capturing Chinese protesters and tying them up with ropes. The text reads (translated from Chinese): They are going to kill us with the rough and big ropes they use to capture us with.


From peace to protests

The students of Chung Cheng High and Chinese High School had tried to submit separate petitions to the government. These petitions had all their reasons, along with a peaceful request for exemption from National Service. Unfortunately, these petitions ended up getting ignored by the government.
The riot police, on the other hand, made decisions that were frankly uncalled for. They gave the students a few warnings before engaging them with force. We must not forget that these are young boys and girls who were peacefully protesting. While this may be a protocol to use in the case of a violent and aggressive mob, engaging these innocent students who had no intention of violence was unnecessary and added fuel to the fire.





Furthermore, a lack of information on National Service caused many to speculate that they would be conscripted to fight in Malayan jungles for the British. Both parties had acceptable reasons for their attitudes towards the National Service Ordinance, so this conflict was avoidable. Who, then, was truly at fault for this needless suffering? We should first understand the rest of the events.

Learning points

All in all, if the riot police had not been so quick to attack the students, this violence could have been easily avoided. The government and the police had the power to peacefully resolve this issue, but did not. Now, blood has been needlessly shed, the problem has been exacerbated and we can expect even more riots. The authorities should change their perception of students and treat them with more respect if they want to avoid catastrophes like these.

On 13 May, the students assembled on Clemenceau Avenue to protest. After their petitions got rejected, they felt that they needed their voices heard somehow, and therefore simply started a peaceful protest. The government once again ignored their pleas and the riot police were deployed. After a few warnings, they resorted to force to disperse the students. Students who marched up to Penang Road just to show moral support were handled the same way. As a result, 20 students and 6 police officers were injured.
